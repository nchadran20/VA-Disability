VA Disability Rating for Back Pain & Lumbar Spine

Updated: January 2026 Read time: 9 min

Low back pain and lumbar spine conditions are among the most commonly claimed VA disabilities — and among the most commonly underrated. Veterans frequently receive 10% when their condition warrants 20%, 40%, or higher. Understanding exactly how the VA measures your back condition is the key to getting the rating you deserve.

💡 The Critical Point

The VA rates lumbar spine conditions almost entirely based on range of motion measurements taken during your C&P exam. If your worst days aren't captured in that exam, your rating will be too low. Keep reading to understand why — and what to do about it.

How the VA Rates the Lumbar Spine

Lumbar spine conditions are rated under Diagnostic Code 5235-5243 using the General Rating Formula for Diseases and Injuries of the Spine. The primary measurement is forward flexion — how far you can bend forward at the waist.

10%Greater than 60° but with painOR muscle spasm/guarding without abnormal spine contour$180.42
20%Greater than 30° but not more than 60°OR muscle spasm on extreme motion with abnormal gait$356.71
40%30° or lessOR favorable ankylosis of the entire thoracolumbar spine$796.05
50%Unfavorable ankylosis of entire thoracolumbar spine$1,133.68
100%Unfavorable ankylosis of entire spine$3,938.58

The Flare-Up Problem

Here's where most veterans get hurt: the C&P exam happens on one specific day. If that day happens to be a relatively good day for your back — which is common because many veterans push through pain — the examiner measures better range of motion than your typical experience. Your rating reflects that one measurement, not your actual daily reality.

The VA is required to consider flare-ups — periods when your condition is significantly worse. You have the right to tell the examiner how your range of motion is affected during a flare-up, and the examiner is supposed to note this. If they don't ask, volunteer the information.

Say something like: "On my worst days, which happen several times a week, I can only bend forward about 15 degrees before the pain stops me." This statement, if noted in the exam, can support a 40% rating even if your measured flexion that day was 45 degrees.

Intervertebral Disc Syndrome (IVDS) — A Separate Rating Opportunity

If your back condition involves intervertebral disc syndrome — herniated discs, degenerative disc disease, or similar — you may qualify for an additional rating based on the number of incapacitating episodes you experience per year. This rating runs alongside your range of motion rating, and the VA uses whichever is higher.

RatingIncapacitating Episodes Per Year
10%At least one episode of at least 1 week but less than 2 weeks
20%At least one episode of at least 2 weeks but less than 4 weeks
40%At least one episode of at least 4 weeks but less than 6 weeks
60%At least one episode of at least 6 weeks

An "incapacitating episode" means a period when your doctor prescribed bed rest or treatment. Keep records of every time your back forces you to rest, miss work, or seek treatment. These records directly support a higher IVDS rating.

Secondary Conditions From Back Injuries

A lumbar spine condition can cause or contribute to several secondary conditions, each with its own rating:

  • Radiculopathy — nerve pain radiating into the legs. Rated separately for each leg (10–60% per extremity depending on severity)
  • Hip conditions — altered gait from back pain can damage hip joints over time
  • Knee conditions — same mechanism as hip damage
  • Erectile dysfunction — nerve compression from lumbar conditions can cause ED, qualifying for SMC-K ($139.87/month added)
  • Depression/anxiety — chronic pain conditions frequently cause secondary mental health conditions
📋 Action Item

If you have radiculopathy — tingling, numbness, or pain shooting down your legs — file it as a separate secondary condition immediately. Many veterans with 20% back ratings add 20–40% for radiculopathy and see their combined rating jump significantly.

What to Do at Your C&P Exam

  • Don't warm up or stretch before the exam — arrive in your typical daily condition
  • Describe your worst days, not your best
  • Explicitly mention flare-ups and how they limit your motion
  • Describe functional loss — difficulty sitting, standing, bending, lifting, sleeping
  • Mention any assistive devices you use (cane, back brace, TENS unit)
  • Note how the condition affects your work and daily activities

Bottom Line

Back conditions are frequently underrated because the measurement happens on one day and doesn't capture the full picture. Know your range of motion numbers, document your flare-ups, mention IVDS episodes, and file every secondary condition. The difference between a 10% and 40% back rating is over $600 per month — and that's before adding radiculopathy.
