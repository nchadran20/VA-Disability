Sleep Apnea Secondary to PTSD: Step-by-Step 2026 Guide

Updated: June 2026 Read time: 9 min 2026 Rates Applied

Sleep apnea secondary to PTSD is one of the most valuable secondary VA claims available — and the scientific evidence supporting the connection is strong. A successful secondary sleep apnea claim adds a 50% rating ($1,075.16/month) to whatever rating you already have. Here's the step-by-step process for 2026.

💰 What This Claim Is Worth

If you have 70% PTSD and add 50% sleep apnea secondary, your new combined rating reaches approximately 85% — worth $2,297.96/month alone in 2026, up from $1,760.58. That's an additional $537/month, $6,444/year, completely tax-free. Use our combined rating calculator to see your exact new combined rating.

The Scientific Connection Between PTSD and Sleep Apnea

The connection is medically well-established:

  • PTSD causes hyperarousal and disrupts normal sleep architecture, increasing upper airway muscle tone dysregulation during sleep
  • Studies show veterans with PTSD have 2–4 times higher rates of sleep apnea than those without PTSD
  • PTSD-related weight changes (often gain due to inactivity, medications, stress eating) contribute to obstructive sleep apnea development
  • PTSD medications, particularly some antidepressants, can worsen sleep-disordered breathing

This scientific basis makes nexus letters for this connection relatively straightforward for knowledgeable physicians to write.

Step-by-Step Filing Process

  1. Get diagnosed with sleep apnea — A sleep study (polysomnography) confirming obstructive sleep apnea is the gold standard. A home sleep test may be sufficient. Your doctor must document the diagnosis.
  2. Get a CPAP prescription — This establishes the 50% rating criteria automatically. See our CPAP rating guide.
  3. Get a nexus letter — Your sleep physician or treating doctor writes a letter stating: "It is at least as likely as not that [veteran]'s obstructive sleep apnea is caused by or aggravated by their service-connected PTSD." See our nexus letter guide for exactly what this letter must contain.
  4. File VA Form 21-526EZ — List sleep apnea as a new condition secondary to your service-connected PTSD
  5. Attend your C&P exam — The VA will schedule an exam. Bring your sleep study results, CPAP prescription, and nexus letter. See our C&P exam guide.

If the VA Denies the Secondary Connection

Denials of sleep apnea secondary to PTSD are common but frequently overturned on appeal. Common denial reasons and how to counter them:

  • "No nexus established" — Get a stronger private nexus letter that specifically cites the medical literature linking PTSD to sleep apnea
  • "Examiner found no nexus" — A private nexus letter is competing evidence and should trigger a new examination or rating decision
  • "Condition pre-existed service" — Sleep apnea rarely has documented pre-service history; push back on this reasoning

See our appeal guide and appeal options comparison for next steps if denied.
