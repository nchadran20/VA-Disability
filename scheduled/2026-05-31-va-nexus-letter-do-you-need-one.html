Do You Need a Nexus Letter for Your VA Claim? (2026 Guide)

Updated: May 2026 Read time: 9 min 2026 Rates Applied

A nexus letter — a medical opinion connecting your condition to your service — is one of the most powerful tools in a VA disability claim. But not every claim needs one, and understanding when you do and don't need one saves time and money. Here's the complete 2026 guide.

📋 Nexus Letter Fast Facts

A nexus letter is a written medical opinion from a doctor stating it is "at least as likely as not" that your condition is connected to military service. For our complete guide on what nexus letters contain and how to get one, see our nexus letter guide. This article covers specifically when you need one.

When You Do NOT Need a Nexus Letter

  • Presumptive conditions — Agent Orange, Gulf War, PACT Act, and other presumptive conditions don't require proving service connection — the VA presumes it. See our Agent Orange guide and Gulf War guide.
  • Clear in-service event with current diagnosis — If your service medical records document the exact injury and you have a current diagnosis of the same condition, the nexus may be self-evident from the records
  • Conditions diagnosed in service — If the VA diagnosed your condition while you were in service, service connection may be straightforward from records alone
  • Combat injuries with documented treatment — Well-documented combat injuries with clear current residuals often don't need additional nexus documentation

When You Almost Always Need a Nexus Letter

  • Secondary conditions — Any condition you're claiming as secondary to another service-connected condition requires a medical opinion linking the two conditions. See our secondary conditions guide.
  • Conditions not documented in service records — If you have a current condition but it wasn't noted in your service medical records, you need a doctor to explain how service could have caused it anyway
  • Mental health conditions not formally diagnosed in service — PTSD, depression, anxiety often benefit from a nexus letter connecting in-service stressors to the current diagnosis
  • Conditions with long latency — Diseases that develop years after the causative exposure (like many cancers) need medical explanation of the delay

The "At Least As Likely As Not" Standard

A nexus letter must contain the magic phrase: it is "at least as likely as not" that the condition is connected to service. This is a 50% probability standard — not "definitely" or "certainly." The phrase matters legally. A letter that says "possibly related" or "may have been caused by" is weaker than one using the exact VA standard language.

Private Nexus Letters vs VA C&P Exams

The VA will conduct its own C&P exam as part of your claim — and the examiner's opinion functions as the VA's nexus determination. A private nexus letter from your own doctor serves as competing evidence. When a VA C&P examiner's opinion is negative, a strong private nexus letter can be the difference between denial and approval.
