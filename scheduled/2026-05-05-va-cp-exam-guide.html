VA C&P Exam: What to Expect and How to Prepare (2026)

Updated: April 2026 Read time: 9 min 2026 Rates Applied

The Compensation and Pension (C&P) exam is often the most important appointment in your VA disability claim — and the one most veterans are least prepared for. A good C&P exam can result in the rating you deserve. A bad one — or being unprepared — can cost you hundreds of dollars per month for years. Here's exactly what to expect and how to prepare.

📋 C&P Exam Fast Facts

What it is: A medical examination conducted by a VA provider to assess the severity of your claimed conditions. Who conducts it: VA or contract medical provider. How long: 30 minutes to 2 hours depending on conditions claimed. What it determines: The rating percentage the VA assigns to each of your conditions.

What Actually Happens at a C&P Exam

The examiner reviews your service records and medical records, then examines you and asks about your symptoms. For physical conditions, they measure range of motion, test strength, and assess function. For mental health conditions, they conduct a structured interview about symptoms and how they affect daily life. The examiner then writes a Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ) that becomes the primary evidence for your rating.

The Single Most Important Thing to Do

Describe your worst days — not your average days, and definitely not your best days. The VA is supposed to rate your condition at its worst, but examiners sometimes capture only how you feel in the moment. If you're having a good day at the exam, explicitly say "this is a better day than usual — my typical week involves [describe worst symptoms]."

What to Bring to Your C&P Exam

  • A written list of all your symptoms for each condition — organized by condition
  • Notes describing how each condition affects your daily life, work, sleep, and relationships
  • Any relevant medical records not already in your VA file
  • A personal statement describing your worst-day experiences
  • Buddy statements from family members or coworkers if you have them
  • Your nexus letter if claiming secondary conditions

Common Mistakes That Cost Veterans Their Rating

  • Minimizing symptoms — Military culture says "suck it up." The C&P exam is the wrong place for that instinct.
  • Only describing good days — If you can function normally today but are bedridden two days per week, say so explicitly.
  • Not mentioning all symptoms — The examiner only rates what they document. If you forget to mention a symptom, it may not appear in your rating.
  • Agreeing with everything the examiner says — If the examiner states something inaccurate, politely correct them.
  • Taking pain medication before the exam — This can mask your true functional limitations during range of motion testing.

After the Exam — What Happens Next

  1. The examiner completes their DBQ within a few days to weeks
  2. You can request a copy of the DBQ through your VA records
  3. The VA uses the DBQ to assign your rating — typically 2–6 months after the exam
  4. If the DBQ contains errors or omissions, you can request a supplemental exam or file evidence challenging the findings

If Your C&P Exam Was Inadequate

An exam is legally "inadequate" if the examiner failed to address all claimed conditions, didn't review your service records, or provided conclusions without supporting rationale. File a supplemental claim or request a new exam if yours was inadequate. See our appeal guide for the full process.
