Supplemental Claim vs Higher-Level Review vs BVA: Which to Choose (2026)

Updated: May 2026 Read time: 9 min 2026 Rates Applied

When the VA denies your claim or rates you too low, you have exactly three options — and choosing the wrong one can slow your case by years. Here's exactly how to decide which path is right for your situation.

⚡ The Decision in 30 Seconds

Have new evidence (new nexus letter, new medical records, new buddy statements)? → Supplemental Claim. VA made a clear error with existing evidence? → Higher-Level Review. Complex legal issues, long-term fight, want a hearing? → Board of Veterans Appeals. Not sure? → Supplemental Claim is usually the safest choice. Supplemental Claim — The Most Commonly Used

A Supplemental Claim adds new and relevant evidence to a previously decided claim. "New and relevant" means evidence that wasn't in your file before and that relates to your claimed condition. Common examples:

  • A new nexus letter from a private doctor (see our nexus letter guide)
  • New medical records showing worsened condition or new diagnosis
  • Buddy statements not previously submitted
  • Private medical opinion that contradicts the VA examiner's opinion

Higher-Level Review — For Clear VA Errors

The HLR is best when the VA had all the evidence but applied the rating criteria incorrectly. Examples of clear errors appropriate for HLR:

  • VA applied the wrong diagnostic code to your condition
  • The C&P examiner's opinion was "conclusory" — reached a conclusion without adequate explanation
  • VA didn't consider all your submitted evidence
  • Math error in calculating your combined rating

See our detailed Higher-Level Review guide for the full process.

Board of Veterans Appeals — The Long Game

The BVA is an independent tribunal separate from the VA regional office. It takes much longer (1–5+ years) but offers:

  • A formal hearing before a Veterans Law Judge
  • The ability to submit new evidence (in Direct Review and Evidence Submission lanes)
  • Access to the Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims if the BVA denies you

The BVA is typically reserved for cases where you've exhausted faster options or have complex legal arguments. See our complete appeal guide for more context.
