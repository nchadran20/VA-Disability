VSO vs VA Disability Attorney: Which Should You Use? (2026)

Updated: May 2026 Read time: 9 min 2026 Rates Applied

One of the most common questions veterans ask is whether to hire an attorney to handle their VA disability claim — or use a free VSO. Here's the honest answer, including when each option makes sense and what the real costs are.

💰 The Bottom Line Upfront

For initial claims: Always start with a free VSO. Filing a claim is free and VSOs provide excellent assistance at no cost. For appeals, especially at the Board of Veterans Appeals level: An experienced VA accredited attorney may significantly improve your outcome. The contingency fee (typically 20–33%) only applies to back pay — not future monthly payments.

VSO (Veterans Service Organizations) — Free Help

VSOs are accredited organizations that help veterans file and manage VA disability claims at no cost. Major VSOs include:

  • DAV (Disabled American Veterans) — Largest and most well-resourced VSO
  • VFW (Veterans of Foreign Wars) — Strong national presence
  • American Legion — Widespread local chapters
  • Vietnam Veterans of America (VVA) — Focused on Vietnam-era veterans
  • State VA offices — Every state has free veterans service staff

VSOs can help you: file initial claims, gather evidence, prepare for C&P exams, and file Supplemental Claims and Higher-Level Reviews. See our HLR guide and appeal guide for the process VSOs can help with.

VA Accredited Attorneys — When They Add Value

VA accredited attorneys specialize in veterans disability law and can represent you before the Board of Veterans Appeals and the Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims. They cannot charge upfront fees — all fees are contingency based (percentage of back pay awarded).

When an attorney makes sense:

  • Your claim has been denied multiple times and you believe you have a legitimate case
  • You're filing at the BVA level where legal arguments matter more
  • Your case involves complex legal issues (effective dates, clear and unmistakable error, etc.)
  • The back pay amount at stake is substantial enough to justify the contingency fee

Attorney Fees — What They Actually Cost

VA accredited attorneys work on contingency — they receive a percentage of any back pay awarded, typically 20–33%. Critically: they cannot charge fees on future monthly payments — only on the lump sum back pay from your effective date to the decision date. If you're awarded $50,000 in back pay and the attorney charges 25%, they receive $12,500. Your future monthly payments are never touched by attorney fees.

Claims Assistance Companies — Be Cautious

There are many for-profit companies advertising VA claim help. By federal law, no one can charge you to file an initial VA claim. If a company charges upfront fees for filing assistance, this is illegal. Only VA-accredited attorneys and claims agents can legally charge fees — and only after a favorable decision, on back pay only. When in doubt, use a free VSO.
